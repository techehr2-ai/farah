\begin{abstract}
Multilingual large language models (LLMs) are
increasingly used for open-ended text generation, yet
their behaviour in low-resource languages such as
Urdu remains poorly understood. To
study this, we curate \textbf{Urdu-Stories}, a dataset
of  93 stories generated with three contemporary LLMs
(GPT-5.1, Qwen-3-Max, DeepSeek-3.1). All stories are
manually annotated by five native Urdu speakers under a
nine-label taxonomy covering linguistic, semantic, and
cultural / script dimensions.Our analysis surfaces five
consistent findings: (1) \textit{memorisation over
creativity}: for well-known literary titles, models
often reproduce the original story rather than
generating a new plot; (2) \textit{unnatural title
anchoring}: we introduce the Title Repetition
Rate (TRR) to capture a behaviour where models
repeatedly insert the given title into the body of the
story to signal what it is ``about'', exceeding
original-author TRR; (3) \textit{linguistic fragility}:
; (4) \textit{semantic and register
instability}: factual contradictions,
grammatical-but-meaningless sentences, and unprompted
Punjabi switching; and (5) \textit{cultural
shallowness}: pervasive cross-script contamination
 and a 3.7:1
male-default protagonist bias . Together, these results show that current
multilingual LLMs remain fluent but neither creative
nor culturally grounded in Urdu. We release the
dataset, annotations, and evaluation scripts to
support further research on open-ended generation in
low-resource languages.
\end{abstract}


\section{Introduction}

Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable performance across a wide range of text generation tasks, yet this success remains concentrated predominantly in English and a few other handful of languages. While many of these models are described as multilingual and are capable of generating text in numerous languages, their performance in most of these languages is insufficiently evaluated. This gap is especially pronounced for low-resource languages, which collectively represent a substantial portion of the world’s linguistic diversity.

Evaluating language competence demands more than measuring syntax, semantics and morphology or fluency of the generated text. Language is inseparable from the cultural values, social norms and shared historical experiences. Consequently, a reliable language model must internalize these nuances to serve reliably as a tool for information retrieval and content generation. Without such grounding, generated content may appear superficially fluent while remaining semantically unstable, culturally shallow, or socially misaligned.

In this work, we question how well contemporary multilingual LLMs are grounded in the linguistic and cultural dimensions of low-resource languages. We take Urdu as our case study and investigate the capacity of LLMs to produce stories that are linguistically correct, semantically meaningful, and culturally situated within the Pakistani context. Story generation is a deliberate design choice, unlike factual or short-form tasks, narrative generation demands contextual awareness, structural coherence, and cultural sensitivity sustained over longer text, making it a particularly revealing testbed for model capability.

Concretely, we examine whether LLM-generated Urdu stories preserve contextual relevance, avoid formulaic narrative patterns, and handle culturally sensitive themes appropriately, whether those themes originate from Urdu literary traditions or are adapted from Western sources. This leads us to the following research questions:


\begin{itemize}
\item Do LLMs generate original Urdu narratives, or do they reproduce existing storytelling patterns?
\item How linguistically accurate are LLM-generated Urdu stories in terms of grammar, spelling, and sentence structure?
\item Are the generated stories semantically coherent and contextually meaningful?
\item To what extent do LLM-generated stories preserve Pakistani cultural and social contexts?
\item Do LLM-generated Urdu stories exhibit gender bias in character representation and narrative focus?
\end{itemize}


To study these questions, we introduce \textbf{Urdu-Stories}, a dataset of 93 stories generated using three contemporary LLMs: GPT-5.1, Qwen-3-Max, and DeepSeek-3.1. All stories are manually annotated by five native Urdu speakers using a nine-label taxonomy covering linguistic, semantic, cultural, and script-related dimensions. Our analysis reveals five consistent findings. First, models exhibit \textit{memorisation over creativity}: when prompted with well-known literary titles, they frequently reproduce the original story rather than generating a novel narrative. Second, we observe \textit{unnatural title anchoring}: models repeatedly insert the provided title into the story body to signal topical relevance. To quantify this behaviour, we introduce the \textit{Title Repetition Rate (TRR)}, which substantially exceeds the repetition patterns found in human-written stories. Third, we identify \textit{linguistic fragility}, including spelling inconsistencies and grammatical instability. Fourth, models demonstrate \textit{semantic and register instability}, producing factual contradictions, grammatically valid but meaningless sentences, and unprompted switching into Punjabi. Finally, we observe \textit{cultural shallowness}, reflected in pervasive cross-script contamination and a 3.7:1 male-default protagonist bias.

Together, these findings demonstrate that current multilingual LLMs remain fluent in Urdu at the surface level, yet fail to achieve deeper creativity, semantic robustness, and cultural grounding.\footnote{We release the dataset, annotations, and evaluation scripts to support future research on open-ended generation in low-resource languages.}
Broadly, our findings carry implications beyond Urdu. We believe that our findings will be consistent with many low-resource languages if not all. They highlight a need of substantial and dedicated efforts in development models for low-resource languages.

\subsection{Story Title Corpus}
\label{subsec:title_corpus}

We constructed a corpus of \textbf{31 story titles} drawn
from four different sources
(Table~\ref{tab:title_distribution}): \emph{Translated Short
Story Titles} (TSST, N~=~11) from canonical English short
fiction (e.g.\ Jackson, Chekhov, Hemingway, Maupassant);
\emph{Urdu Short Story Titles} (UST, N~=~11) from canonical
twentieth-century Urdu writers used \emph{verbatim}
(e.g.\ Manto, Chughtai, Ghulam Abbas, Bedi, Hyder);
\emph{Fables} (N~=~3), short Indo-Pakistani moral tales used
as a memorisation control; and \emph{Neutral Urdu Titles}
(NUT, N~=~6), author-original Urdu titles composed by the
research team as an unseen-prompt control. The complete
title list with authors, sources, and ALA-LC transliterations
is given in Appendix~\ref{app:titles}
(Table~\ref{tab:title_full_list}).

For TSST, we obtained an initial Urdu rendering of each
English title via the GPT-5.1 translation API and then
manually verified every translation with a native Urdu
speaker; published Urdu renderings (e.g.\
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{لاٹری}, \foreignlanguage{arabic}{بندر کا پنجہ})
were retained verbatim where they exist.

The four-way split is intentional: TSST tests cross-cultural

depth required for sentence-level human annotation.

\subsection{Generation Protocol}
\label{subsec:generation}

Three LLMs were evaluated: \textbf{GPT}
(\texttt{5.1}), \textbf{QWEN} (\texttt{3-Max}),
and \textbf{DeepSeek} (\texttt{3.1}), all accessed
in \texttt{<11-2025>}. Each title was issued to every model
exactly once using a fixed zero-shot prompt:

\begin{quote}\itshape
``Write a 2000-word Urdu story in a Pakistani cultural
context. The title is \foreignlanguage{arabic}{دوسری عورت}.''
\end{quote}

Few-shot exemplars and chain-of-thought scaffolding were
deliberately omitted so that observed differences reflect
model capacity rather than prompt engineering.
\subsection{Corpus Statistics}
\label{subsec:corpus_stats}

The protocol produced \textbf{93 stories} (31 titles
$\times$ 3 models) totalling \textbf{151{,}965 words}

\subsection{Annotation and Inter-Annotator Agreement}
\label{subsec:annotation}

Five native Urdu speakers annotated all 93 stories in
Label Studio using nine labels in four categories
(Table~\ref{tab:labels_compact}). Most labels are applied
at the \emph{paragraph} level; entity labels (\textbf{PER},
\textbf{City}, \textbf{MaleProtagonist},
\textbf{FemaleProtagonist}) are applied once per
\emph{story}. \emph{Obliqueness} (gender / number /
oblique-case agreement, e.g.\ \foreignlanguage{arabic}{سا/سی},
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{اتنا/اتنی}) is treated as a sub-type of
\emph{Grammar Error}; \emph{Semantic Anomaly} marks sentences that
are syntactically well-formed but semantically empty;
\emph{Context Mismatch} marks factual contradictions; and
\emph{Foreign Word} marks any token (whole or partial)
containing non-Urdu script characters or non-Urdu words.

A stratified subset of \textbf{60 stories} (20 per model,
balanced across title categories) was independently
re-annotated by a second annotator. Cohen's $\kappa$ was
computed using exact text+label match for PER/City,
substring-tolerant match for protagonist labels (to
accommodate annotators selecting the name token vs.\ the
introducing phrase), and same-label-in-same-paragraph
match for all other labels. Overall agreement was
$\kappa$~=~\textbf{xxx} (\textbf{xxx} reliability); per-model
and per-label scores are reported in
Appendix~\ref{app:iaa}.

\section{Qualitative Analysis}
Several models generated grammatically correct Urdu but failed to preserve culturally authentic Pakistani dialogues and naming conventions.


\subsection{Linguistic and Semantic Quality Analysis}

Manual annotation of all 93 generated stories 
(31 titles $\times$ 3 models) identified 
\textbf{762 quality issues} across three dimensions: 
linguistic errors (N~=~477, 62.6\%), semantic 
coherence failures (N~=~277, 36.4\%), and cultural 
mismatches (N~=~8, 1.0\%). Two automatically 
detected error types --- typographic dash misuse 
and foreign word intrusion --- are reported 
separately in Section~\ref{subsec:auto_errors}. 
Table~\ref{tab:error_summary} (Appendix) presents 
the complete breakdown.

%─────────────────────────────────────────
\subsubsection{Linguistic Errors}
%─────────────────────────────────────────

Linguistic errors constituted the largest share 
of identified issues (N~=~1359). Four subtypes 
were annotated: grammatical mistakes, spelling 
errors, space deletion, and em-dash.

\textbf{Grammatical errors} were the most 
frequent category overall (N~=~316, avg~=~3.39 
per story). QWEN exhibited the highest rate 
(N~=~138), followed by 
DeepSeek (N~=~106) and GPT (N~=~72) . Recurring patterns included 
incorrect postposition usage (\foreignlanguage{arabic}{نے}، \foreignlanguage{arabic}{کو}،  
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{سے}), broken verb-subject agreement, and 
malformed compound sentences --- errors that 
significantly impair readability in Urdu prose.
To characterise the kinds of grammatical failures
observed, we discuss three representative examples,
each highlighting a different fault line in Urdu
syntax.


\textit{(i) Ergative case marker omission.}
In QWEN's
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{زرین خاموشی سے بندوق نیچے رکھ دی}
(\textit{Zarīn khāmōshī sē bandūq nīchē rakh dī},
``Zarin quietly put the gun down''), the agent
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{زرین} requires the ergative
postposition \foreignlanguage{arabic}{نے}
(\textit{nē}) because the verb
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{رکھ دی} (\textit{rakh dī}) is
transitive and perfective: the correct form is
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{زرین نے خاموشی سے بندوق نیچے رکھ دی}
(\textit{Zarīn nē khāmōshī sē bandūq nīchē rakh dī}).
The same omission recurs in GPT's
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{وہ آسمان کی طرف دیکھ کر گھوڑے کو موڑا}
(\textit{Wuh āsmān kī ṭaraf dēkh kar ghōṛē kō mōṛā},
missing \foreignlanguage{arabic}{اس نے} \textit{us nē}).
Ergative \foreignlanguage{arabic}{نے} marking is a
hallmark of Urdu morphosyntax with no English analogue,
and its absence suggests cross-lingual transfer from a
nominative-only template.

\textit{(ii) Gender agreement violation.}
In GPT's
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{اس کے منہ سے آج کوئی غرور بھرا بھونک نہیں نکل رہا تھا}
(\textit{Us kē munh sē āj kōʾī ghurūr bharā bhaunk
nahīṅ nikal rahā thā}, ``no proud bark came out of his
mouth today''), the noun
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{بھونک} (\textit{bhaunk},
\emph{bark}) is feminine in Urdu and triggers feminine
agreement on both the modifier and the verb: the
corrected form is
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{غرور بھری بھونک  نہیں نکل رہی تھی}
(\textit{ghurūr bharī bhaunk \ldots nahīṅ nikal rahī
thī}). The model nevertheless propagates masculine
agreement through the entire clause, indicating that
grammatical gender is being assigned by surface
frequency rather than by lexical entry.

\textit{(iii) Aspect mismatch in sequential narrative.}
QWEN's
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{اگلے دن، وہ سویرے اٹھا، نہاتا دھوتا، اوور کوٹ پہنا اور دفتر پہنچ گیا}
(\textit{Aglē din, wuh sawērē uṭhā, nahātā dhōtā, ōvar
kōṭ pahnā aur daftar pahuṅch gayā}, ``the next day, he
got up early, bathed, put on the overcoat, and reached
the office'') chains four events in a single sentence
using past-perfective forms
(\foreignlanguage{arabic}{اٹھا} \textit{uṭhā},
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{پہنا} \textit{pahnā},
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{پہنچ گیا} \textit{pahuṅch
gayā}), but inserts
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{نہاتا دھوتا} (\textit{nahātā
dhōtā}, habitual imperfective). The corrected form is
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{نہایا دھویا} (\textit{nahāyā
dhōyā}). This aspectual slip is particularly
diagnostic because it occurs within an otherwise
well-formed sequence, suggesting that the model can
produce isolated verb forms but struggles to maintain
aspectual consistency across a narrative clause.

These examples show that grammatical errors in
LLM-generated Urdu rarely look like typos or careless
slips. Instead, they cluster around the parts of Urdu
grammar that have no direct English equivalent: marking
who did what (case), making words match in gender, and
keeping the timing of events consistent across a
sentence. A fluent Urdu reader notices such errors
immediately, because they affect how the sentence
\emph{feels}, not just how it reads. Their recurrence
across all three models suggests that these are not
random mistakes but reflect a deeper gap in how Urdu
morphosyntax is represented during generation.


\textbf{Agreement Error} A substantial share of grammatical issues
( \textit{cf.}\
Table~\ref{tab:error_summary}) involved local
agreement failures within a 2--3 word window. We
distinguish two sub-types. \textit{Gender agreement
errors} arise when an adjective, verb, or possessive
fails to match the gender of its head noun in a
nominative context (e.g.\
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{عجیب سا ہلچل} for feminine
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{ہلچل}); these are the most
common in our data. \textit{Oblique-case agreement
errors} (\emph{obliqueness}, in the strict
linguistic sense) arise when a masculine singular
noun or adjective fails to take its oblique form
before a postposition such as
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{نے},
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{کو},
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{سے}, or
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{کی}
(e.g.\ \foreignlanguage{arabic}{سر کٹا سوار نے}
$\rightarrow$ \foreignlanguage{arabic}{سر کٹے سوار نے}).
Both sub-types share a common signature: they are
mechanical, strictly local, and detectable within a
short window, suggesting that a lightweight
post-editing step over noun-phrase windows could
plausibly correct a large share of them without
modifying the underlying LLM.

\textbf{Spelling errors} (N~=~108, avg~=~1.16 
per story) were distributed across all models 
(DeepSeek: 52, GPT: 16, QWEN: 40). Their 
relatively uniform distribution suggests that 
spelling degradation is model-agnostic and 
may reflect tokenization artifacts in Urdu 
Nastaliq script processing rather than 
model-specific weaknesses. 
Four recurring
patterns characterise these errors.

\textit{(i) Hamza omission in derived adjectives.}
QWEN produced \foreignlanguage{arabic}{دریاوی}
(\textit{daryāwī}) and \foreignlanguage{arabic}{جادوی}
(\textit{jādwī}) where the correct forms are
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{دریائی} (\textit{daryāʾī},
``riverine'') and \foreignlanguage{arabic}{جادوئی}
(\textit{jāduʾī}, ``magical''). Both errors drop the
hamza-on-yē that Urdu uses to mark the adjectival
suffix on vowel-final stems. The fact that two
unrelated stems show the same omission suggests a
systematic gap in modelling Urdu's hamza-bearing
morphology rather than incidental misspellings. A
related but inverted failure appears in DeepSeek's
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{مسئبت} (\textit{musʾibat})
for \foreignlanguage{arabic}{مصیبت}
(\textit{muṣībat}, ``calamity''), where a hamza is
\emph{inserted} into an Arabic loanword that does not
take one. Together these cases show that hamza
placement, governed by complex morphological and
etymological rules in Urdu, is unstable in current
LLMs in both directions.

\textit{(ii) Vowel and diacritic confusion.}
DeepSeek produced \foreignlanguage{arabic}{میچس}
(\textit{mīchas}) instead of
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{ماچس} (\textit{māchis},
``matchbox''), substituting the short-\textit{i} vowel
sign for the long-\textit{ā} alef. Similarly,
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{مینہہ} (\textit{mīnh}) was
produced for \foreignlanguage{arabic}{مینہ}
(\textit{mēnh}, ``rain'') with a duplicated final
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{ہ}, and QWEN produced
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{ہیریو} (\textit{hīriyō}) for
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{ہیرو} (\textit{hīrō},
``hero'') by inserting a spurious
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{ی}. Such errors closely
resemble the orthographic mistakes of a learner who
has heard the word but not internalised its standard
written form.

\textit{(iii) Visually-similar character substitution.}
GPT produced \foreignlanguage{arabic}{جھلے ہوئے ہاتھ}
(\textit{jhalē hūʾē hāth}) where the intended phrase
is \foreignlanguage{arabic}{جلے ہوئے ہاتھ}
(\textit{jalē hūʾē hāth}, ``burnt hands''),
substituting \foreignlanguage{arabic}{جھ} (\textit{jh},
aspirated) for \foreignlanguage{arabic}{ج}
(\textit{j}). The two graphemes differ only by a
diacritic dot cluster, and the substitution changes
both pronunciation and meaning. We attribute these
errors to byte-pair tokenisation of Nastaliq script,
where visually adjacent characters can share token
neighbourhoods despite phonetic distance.

\textit{(iv) English-mediated retroflex confusion.}
Two errors point to a deeper cross-lingual effect.
DeepSeek produced \foreignlanguage{arabic}{ربر}
(\textit{rabar}) for \foreignlanguage{arabic}{ربڑ}
(\textit{rabaṛ}, ``rubber''), \emph{dropping} the
retroflex flap \foreignlanguage{arabic}{ڑ}
(\textit{ṛ}) in favour of the plain trill
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{ر} (\textit{r}). Conversely,
the Punjabi-Urdu given name
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{اللہ دتّہ}
(\textit{Allāh Dittā}) was rendered as
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{اللہ ڈتہ} (\textit{Allāh
Ḍittah}), \emph{inserting} a retroflex
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{ڈ} (\textit{ḍ}) where the
dental \foreignlanguage{arabic}{د} (\textit{d}) is
correct. These errors are diagnostic precisely because
they move in opposite directions: in the first, the
model erases an Urdu phonemic distinction that English
lacks (English ``rubber'' has no retroflex); in the
second, the model overgeneralises the English
transliteration convention by which South Asian dental
\textit{t} and \textit{d} are conventionally written
with retroflex symbols in romanisation. The retroflex
series (\foreignlanguage{arabic}{ٹ},
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{ڈ},
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{ڑ}) is one of the few
phonemic features of Urdu absent from both Arabic and
Persian, and our observations suggest the model
resolves it via an English-mediated phonetic
representation rather than from a native Urdu
inventory.

\textbf{Space deletion}: Space-deletion errors, where two adjacent Urdu
tokens are concatenated into a single orthographic
unit, account for 140 instances across our
93 stories corpus, on average 1.5 per story. Although
the absolute rate is low, comparing each fused form
against its correctly-spaced counterpart reveals a
model-specific orthographic signature. GPT fuses
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{ہو} (\textit{ho},
``be'')\,-auxiliary constructions at substantial
rates (\foreignlanguage{arabic}{ہوگیا}
[\textit{hogayā}, ``became''] 41\%,
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{ہوجاتا}
[\textit{hojātā}, ``would become''] 40\%,
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{ہوگا}
[\textit{hogā}, ``will be''] 67\%), whereas
DeepSeek and QWEN write the same forms with a space
in essentially every instance. The pattern inverts
for \foreignlanguage{arabic}{آ} (\textit{ā},
``come'')\,-auxiliaries: DeepSeek fuses
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{آگیا}
(\textit{āgayā}, ``came'') 44\% and
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{آگئے}
(\textit{āga'e}, ``came [pl.]'') 67\% of the time,
while GPT and QWEN remain near zero. QWEN shows
almost no fusion of either family. Since each model
handles a different closed inventory of compounds
poorly while writing the rest correctly, the errors
reflect sub-word vocabulary choices in each model's
tokeniser rather than a shared weakness in Urdu
generation. Although largely invisible to human
readers, the distinction matters downstream:
whitespace-based Urdu NLP pipelines treat
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{ہوگیا}
(\textit{hogayā}, fused) and
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{ہو گیا}
(\textit{ho gayā}, spaced) as different tokens, and
if LLM output is reused as synthetic training data,
as is increasingly common for low-resource
languages, a model's fusion preferences gain
additional statistical support and risk
self-reinforcing contamination of the language's
digital record. The affected inventory is small and
model-identifiable, so a lookup-based
post-processing step can correct it cheaply. 



\textbf{Em-dash} \label{subsec:auto_errors} Urdu prose 
conventionally does not employ em-dashes (---) 
as structural markers; their 
presence indicates stylistic transfer from 
English writing conventions. Automatic 
detection across all 93 stories identified 
a total of \texttt{623} dash occurrences 
(avg~=~\texttt{6.6} per story), with 
\texttt{21} showing the highest frequency.
Per-model
distribution and per-category breakdown are reported
in Table~\ref{tab:em_dash} (Appendix).
\textbf{xxx} produced the most em-dashes both in
total and per story, consistent with its higher
overall rate of English-stylistic transfer.

\subsubsection{Semantic Coherence}
%─────────────────────────────────────────

Semantic coherence failures (N~=~316, 
avg~=~3.39 per story) constituted the second 
largest error category. 

\textbf{Context mismatch.} Context-mismatch errors arise when a generated
sentence is locally well-formed but conflicts with
earlier narrative content, a real-world fact, or a
culturally established script. They occur in
0.21\% of GPT sentences, 0.44\% of DeepSeek
sentences, and 0.63\% of QWEN sentences. The
absolute rates are low, but the cross-model
ordering matches the QWEN-weakest pattern observed
for grammar and obliqueness (Section
\ref{subsec:linguistic_errors}), suggesting that
narrative coherence is correlated with morphological
precision rather than independent of it. The
annotated instances fall into five recurring failure
modes, each of which reveals a different limitation
of current Urdu LLMs.

\textit{Kinship-term misuse} is the dominant
sub-category. Urdu encodes a much finer-grained
kinship lexicon than English, distinguishing
paternal from maternal relations
(\foreignlanguage{arabic}{دادی} \textit{dādī}
``father's mother'' vs \foreignlanguage{arabic}{نانی}
\textit{nānī} ``mother's mother''), nephews by
sibling gender (\foreignlanguage{arabic}{بھتیجا}
\textit{bhatījā} ``brother's son'' vs
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{بھانجا} \textit{bhānjā}
``sister's son''), and address forms by social
relation. Models routinely collapse these
distinctions: in one QWEN story, a brother writes
to his sister calling her son
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{بھانجا} (\textit{bhānjā},
``sister's son'') where
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{بھتیجا} (\textit{bhatījā},
``brother's son'') is required; a DeepSeek story
introduces a maternal-side visit but uses
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{دادی اماں} (\textit{dādī
ammā}, ``paternal grandmother'') for the
grandmother encountered there. Address forms are
similarly over-generalised: QWEN uses
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{ابا جان} (\textit{abbā
jān}, ``father, respectful'') for an elderly
stranger across multiple stories, and a DeepSeek
wife addresses her husband as
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{بیٹا} (\textit{beṭā},
``son''). These errors are consistent with an
English-mediated representation in which
``uncle'', ``nephew'', ``grandmother'', and
``dear'' map onto whichever Urdu term is
statistically most frequent in the training data,
regardless of the discourse context.

\textit{Long-range gender breakdown} forms a second
cluster, mostly observed in DeepSeek. Once a female
protagonist has been established, the model
nevertheless produces verbs and adjectives in the
masculine: a female speaker uses
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{جانتا} (\textit{jāntā},
masc.\ ``know'') instead of
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{جانتی} (\textit{jāntī},
fem.); a girl on a beach bench is described as
wearing \foreignlanguage{arabic}{عمامہ}
(\textit{`imāmah}, ``turban'', a male head-dress);
two figures including a girl are referred to as
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{دو نوجوان} (\textit{do
nau-jawān}, ``two young men''). These are not local
agreement slips but long-range failures in which
the discourse-established gender is lost over
several sentences.

\textit{Cultural and religious anachronism} appears
in low numbers but high salience. A QWEN story
describes the appearance of the
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{مکمل ہلال} (\textit{mukammal
hilāl}, ``complete crescent'') on Eid, conflating
the crescent (\foreignlanguage{arabic}{ہلال}
\textit{hilāl}) that marks the festival with a full
moon (\foreignlanguage{arabic}{بدر} \textit{badr});
another describes a moon-sighting in the morning. A
DeepSeek story has the remains of a Muslim character
cremated, returned as
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{راکھ} (\textit{rākh},
``ash''), and given a funeral with an empty coffin,
none of which conform to Islamic burial practice. A
GPT story promotes a chef to
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{کیپٹن} (\textit{kepṭan},
``captain''), a term reserved for restaurant
front-of-house staff in Pakistani usage. The models
have memorised Urdu vocabulary but not the cultural
scripts that constrain its use.

\textit{Intra-story contradictions} include
references to a daughter ``lost five years ago''
who is nonetheless still being educated in the same
paragraph (QWEN), a mother said to have sewn a
blanket after her son had already left (GPT), an
uncle described as the protagonist's father's
closest friend (GPT), and the recurring head of a
man ``killed last year'' (DeepSeek). These reveal a
lack of persistent state across sentences in
medium-length narratives.

\textit{Physical and commonsense impossibilities}
round out the category: a shadow with hair, the
colour of a claw being visible in mud, a chandelier
inside a bus, books packed inside a thermos, and a
wrist-watch from 1947 displaying the date 14
November 1971. The surface fluency of these
sentences masks the absence of a world model.

Although Context Mismatch contributes the smallest
percentage of any linguistic-error category, its
qualitative profile is the most diagnostic of how
Urdu LLMs fail. The kinship and cultural sub-types
in particular indicate that current models reproduce
Urdu surface forms while operating with an
English-anchored semantic representation, an
observation consistent with the cross-lingual
contamination patterns reported in our spelling
(Section \ref{subsec:spelling}) and punctuation
sections.

\textbf{Semantic Anamoly} --- 
sentences
that are syntactically well-formed but semantically
impossible or meaningless. Two distinct patterns emerged. 

\textit{(i) Impossible composition.} GPT's
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{آگ کے چراغ سے اُبلتی چائے پی رہا تھا}
(``he was drinking tea boiling from a lamp of fire'')
is grammatically perfect, but the composition
violates real-world plausibility: tea does not boil
from a lamp.

\textit{(ii) Near-synonym confusion.} In DeepSeek's
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{مالک نے میری ایمانداری اور محنت دیکھ کر مجھے بڑھاوا دے دیا ہے},
the intended meaning is ``the master promoted me''
(\foreignlanguage{arabic}{ترقی دے دی}), but the model
substitutes \foreignlanguage{arabic}{بڑھاوا}
(\emph{incitement, encouragement}), yielding ``the
master incited me on seeing my honesty.'' The syntax
is intact and ergative \foreignlanguage{arabic}{نے} is
correctly used, but the lexical choice renders the
sentence semantically incoherent, a failure mode that
fluent Urdu speakers would not produce.



\paragraph{Western-Influenced Context Mismatch.}
A small but qualitatively striking sub-set of
context-mismatch errors involves the substitution
of Pakistani objects, institutions, and practices
with their nearest Western analogues, while the
surrounding Urdu prose remains fluent. Although
these instances are too few to dominate any single
model's overall error rate, they form a coherent
pattern that reinforces the cross-lingual
contamination hypothesis raised in our spelling
and punctuation sections. Three sub-types recur.

\textit{Western material culture in Pakistani
settings.} A GPT story set in a Punjabi village
describes the bedroom wall as covered with
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{پیلا وال پیپر}
(\textit{pīlā wāl pepar}, ``yellow wallpaper''), an
interior-decor convention essentially absent from
rural Pakistani homes, which use lime-wash or
distemper paint. Another GPT story has a rickshaw
driver refer to a
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{گیس اسٹیشن}
(\textit{gais isṭeshan}, ``gas station''), an
American term, where Pakistani usage is uniformly
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{پٹرول پمپ}
(\textit{paṭrol pamp}, ``petrol pump''). A third
introduces a
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{ریفریشمنٹ وین}
(\textit{rifreshmanṭ wain}, ``refreshment van'')
inside a Pakistan-Railways train, where the
corresponding facility is the dining car or the
chai-trolley vendor. A DeepSeek story has a
character produce an old photograph from her
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{پورٹ فولیو}
(\textit{porṭ folīo}, ``portfolio''), a term
associated with professional design or finance
work rather than a personal photo collection.

\textit{Western institutional and role labels.}
DeepSeek refers to a twelve-year-old grandson as a
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{بارہ سالہ جونیئر}
(\textit{bārah sālah jūniyar}, ``twelve-year-old
junior''), importing the American junior--senior
school-grade classification into a Pakistani village
imam's household, where such labels are not used.
The same model also uses
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{ہوٹل کے بوائے}
(\textit{hoṭal ke bo'e}, ``hotel boy'') for a
restaurant waiter, calquing the colonial
``boy''-as-servant register, and introduces a
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{والدین میٹنگ}
(\textit{wāldain mīṭing}, ``parents meeting'') as
a literal translation of the English
``parent--teacher meeting'', whereas the
established Urdu term is
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{پی ٹی ایم}
(\textit{pī ṭī em}).


These cases are vanishing
small in count, but they share a structure that
makes them especially hazardous for downstream use:
the Urdu is correct, the Pakistani reader will
notice the slip immediately, but an automatic
quality filter will not. If such passages are
ingested into the training data of a next-generation
Urdu model, the Western-anchored referent gains
statistical support relative to its Pakistani
counterpart, and the language model's representation
of Pakistani daily life drifts incrementally toward
its English-language source. This is the same
self-reinforcing contamination mechanism we identify
for space-deletion errors
(Section~\ref{subsec:space_deletion}), now operating
at the level of cultural ontology rather than
orthography.

\textbf{Language Register Violations: }A qualitatively distinct failure mode was observed 
exclusively in DeepSeek: the substitution of Urdu 
with Punjabi at the phrase and sentence level, where 
characters switch to Punjabi dialogue within an 
otherwise Urdu narrative. Unlike the orthographic 
errors discussed above, this operates at the 
discourse level and constitutes a direct prompt 
non-compliance, as the generation prompts explicitly 
specified Urdu. The pattern suggests that DeepSeek 
conflates Pakistani linguistic identity with Punjabi, 
the majority regional language of Pakistan, rather 
than maintaining the prompted language boundary.


\subsubsection{Script Contamination in LLM-Generated Urdu Text}

Beyond lexical foreign word intrusion, automated 
Unicode-level analysis revealed a more fundamental 
failure mode: the embedding of non-Urdu script 
characters, including Latin, Devanagari (Hindi), 
Chinese (CJK), and Cyrillic  within generated 
Urdu text. A word was flagged as contaminated if 
any character in it fell outside the Urdu Unicode 
range (U+0600--U+06FF) and its standard presentation 
forms, excluding digits and punctuation. Table 
\ref{tab:script_contamination} summarises findings 
across all 93 stories.

DeepSeek exhibited the most severe contamination 
(61 affected word instances across 15 of 31 stories), 
followed by QWEN (14 instances, 3 stories) and GPT 
(11 instances, 6 stories). Contamination manifested 
in two structural patterns: \textit{pure intrusion}, 
where an entire word belongs to a foreign script, 
and \textit{partial contamination}, where a single 
word contains characters from both Urdu and a foreign 
script, corrupting an otherwise valid Urdu token.

\textbf{Latin Script Intrusion:} Latin script was the most frequent contaminant 
across all models (GPT: 7, QWEN: 13, DeepSeek: 47 
word instances). Pure English word substitution 
included everyday vocabulary for which standard 
Urdu equivalents exist --- \textit{hospital}, 
\textit{career}, \textit{water}, \textit{warmth}, 
\textit{tight}.

Two partial Latin contaminations merit particular 
attention. First, QWEN substituted the pilgrimage 
honorific \foreignlanguage{arabic}{حاجی} with \foreignlanguage{arabic}{ح}aj\foreignlanguage{arabic}{ی} --- replacing 
the vowel graphemes \foreignlanguage{arabic}{ا} and \foreignlanguage{arabic}{ی} with Latin 
\textit{a} and \textit{j} , and repeated this 
malformed token 10 times within a single 
1,343-word story ً, suggesting a 
systematic tokenizer failure rather than an incidental 
error. Second, DeepSeek produced \foreignlanguage{arabic}{کہ}a, where the 
common Urdu verb \foreignlanguage{arabic}{کہا} (\textit{said}) had its 
final character replaced by Latin \textit{a}, a 
substitution that is phonetically equivalent but 
orthographically invalid, indicating that DeepSeek's 
decoder resolved an ambiguous token boundary via 
Latin fallback. Furthermore, \foreignlanguage{arabic}{رسمی}ities, 
an Urdu adjective \foreignlanguage{arabic}{رسمی} (\textit{formal}) 
suffixed with the English plural morpheme 
\textit{-ities}, represents a novel form of 
\textit{cross-lingual morphological contamination} 
not previously documented for Urdu LLM outputs, in 
which English derivational morphology is applied 
to an Urdu stem.

\textbf{Devanagari (Hindi) Intrusion:} Devanagari intrusion was most prevalent in DeepSeek 
 and even present in GPT  and 
QWEN. Given that Urdu and Hindi share 
extensive phonological overlap, this proximity appears to produce 
character-level cross-script leakage, where phonetically 
equivalent Devanagari diacritics substitute for their 
Urdu counterparts e.g.\foreignlanguage{arabic}{ٹھ}ِِ\foreignlanguage{hindi}{ं} \foreignlanguage{arabic}{ڈی}(\textit{cold}) carries the Devanagari 
. More severe is 
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{ک} \foreignlanguage{hindi}{ुत्त} \foreignlanguage{arabic}{وں}, where the Urdu word for 
\textit{dogs} is partially rendered in Devanagari 
mid-token, a morpheme-level script switch rather 
than a diacritic substitution.

\textbf{Chinese and Cyrillic Intrusion:} The most striking contamination instances 
involve Chinese (CJK) and Cyrillic characters 
embedded within Urdu tokens. These  scripts have low/no phonological or typological relationship 
to Urdu, making their presence entirely 
anomalous. 

In both Chinese and Cyrillic instances, the foreign 
token is semantically appropriate in context --- 
\textit{yánsù} (\textit{serious}), 
\textit{de shēnghuó} (\textit{of life}), 
\textit{huàtí} (\textit{topic}); 
\textit{iskrennost'} (\textit{sincerity}) --- 
however, a missing word boundary caused each token 
to attach directly to the preceding Urdu word, 
producing undelimited bilingual compounds. The 
failure is thus orthographic rather than semantic: 
DeepSeek selected a valid lexical item from its 
Chinese or Cyrillic vocabulary where an Urdu 
equivalent was required, and omitted the 
delimiting space. The exception is 
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{پراج} \foreignlanguage{russian}{ек} \foreignlanguage{arabic}{ٹ}, where  
Cyrillic characters are substituted for 
similar Urdu phonemes within a single word.

\subsection{Source-Engagement Analysis}
\subsection{Title Repetition and the Three Modes of Source
            Engagement}
\label{subsec:title_repetition}

A natural question raised by the linguistic and cultural errors of
the previous sections is whether the underlying problem is one of
\emph{language coverage} or one of \emph{text coverage}: does the
model fail because it cannot speak Urdu well, or because it has
never read the specific Urdu texts our titles reference? To
disentangle these we introduce a simple, length-invariant lexical
diagnostic --- the \textbf{Title Repetition Rate (TRR)} --- and
show that it cleanly partitions LLM-generated Urdu stories into
three qualitatively distinct modes of engagement with their source
material, each determined by the visibility of the source text in
the model's training data.


TRR is defined as the number of exact title-phrase occurrences in
the body of a generated story (excluding the opening line),
expressed per 100 words of body text:

\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{TRR} = \frac{\mathrm{count}(\textit{title\_phrase}
                                       \in \textit{story\_body})}
                        {\textit{total\_words}} \times 100
\end{equation}

Both title and story were normalised before matching by removing
diacritics, applying character-variant normalisation, stripping punctuation and normalising whitespace.

\paragraph{Three modes emerge from a single metric.}
Table~\ref{tab:trr_category} shows mean TRR by category and model.
The ordering is the same for every model and spans a tenfold range,
revealing three distinct modes of source engagement.

\begin{description}
  \item[Plot Reproduction (PR)]
    For globally circulated fables,present in the training
    data of all three models, often in multiple languages and in
    children's textbooks worldwide TRR is near zero (TRR $\approx$ 0.06). The model
    retrieves a complete memorised plot (the thirsty crow drops
    pebbles, the greedy dog loses its bone) and has no need to
    re-anchor on the title.
  \item[Thematic Reproduction (TR)] For Western literary classics translated into Urdu (e.g.\
    \foreignlanguage{arabic}{لاٹری}, \foreignlanguage{arabic}{اک گھنٹے کی کہانی}, \foreignlanguage{arabic}{  پیلا وال پیپر}),
    TRR is intermediate 
        (TRR $\approx$ 0.45). The model invokes a remembered \emph{theme}
    --- mental-health breakdown, a wife's brief liberation, sudden
    inversion of fortune --- without retrieving a full plot, and
    consequently uses the title as a partial anchor.
  \item[Lexical Title Unpacking (LT) ]
    For titles drawn from the Urdu literary canon
    (\foreignlanguage{arabic}{لحاف}, \foreignlanguage{arabic}{اوور کوٹ}, \foreignlanguage{arabic}{گرم کوٹ},
    \foreignlanguage{arabic}{ٹھنڈا گوشت}), texts that are largely absent
    from large-scale web pretraining corpora  TRR rises sharply (TRR $\approx$ 0.58, peak 2.79).
    The model has neither plot nor theme to retrieve; the only
    object it can anchor on is the surface meaning of the title
    itself. Chughtai's \foreignlanguage{arabic}{لحاف}, a 1942 short story
    metaphorising hidden lesbian desire, is rendered by QWEN as a
    literal narrative about a quilt with the word
    \foreignlanguage{arabic}{لحاف} repeated 35 times (TRR=2.79).
    Bedi's \foreignlanguage{arabic}{گرم کوٹ} becomes a story about how to
    obtain a warm garment. Manto's \foreignlanguage{arabic}{ٹھنڈا گوشت},
    a story of partition-era trauma and paralysis, becomes a story
    about cold meat.
\end{description}

We refer to this overall pattern as the \textbf{Title Lexicalization
Effect}: in the absence of source-text knowledge, an LLM treats a
metaphorical title as a literal writing prompt, repeating its
constituent words far above the rate any human author of the same
text would use.

Because TRR is computed on raw text rather than on plot content,
the same metric can be applied to the original Urdu stories. We
obtained the source texts of \foreignlanguage{arabic}{لحاف},
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{اوور کوٹ}, \foreignlanguage{arabic}{گرم کوٹ} and
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{ٹھنڈا گوشت} from Rekhta and computed their
TRR as a human baseline (Table~\ref{tab:trr_original}). Original
authors use their title words sparingly (TRR 0.04--0.52), employing
them as thematic anchors rather than as literal subjects.
LLM-generated stories on the same titles exceed the original TRR
in 8 of 9 comparisons (88.9\%), with ratios from 1.4$\times$ to
12.7$\times$. The extreme cases --- QWEN's
\foreignlanguage{arabic}{لحاف} at 5.3$\times$ Chughtai's original,
DeepSeek's \foreignlanguage{arabic}{ٹھنڈا گوشت} at 12.7$\times$
Manto's --- are precisely the canonical Urdu texts most likely to
be absent from training data.

TRR is a purely lexical, length-normalised count: it requires no
LLM judge, no human annotation of story plots, and is therefore
robust to the methodological concerns that affect more
content-sensitive evaluation pipelines. The fact that this
extremely simple metric produces a clean tenfold gradient
(Fable~0.06 $\to$ Urdu~Lit~0.58) and a clean ordering against
the human baseline (LLM~$>$~human in 89\% of cases) makes it
a strong diagnostic for what we argue is the underlying
phenomenon: contemporary LLMs do not fail at Urdu story
generation uniformly. They fail in a way that is sharply
modulated by whether the source text is present in their training
data. For globally circulated fables they reproduce; for Western
classics they paraphrase the theme; for the Urdu literary canon
they fall back on lexical unpacking of the title itself.

\subsection{Creativity vs Memorisation
            for Known Source Texts}
\label{subsec:memorization}

Section~\ref{subsec:title_repetition} established, via title
repetition, that LLM behaviour on Urdu story generation
partitions into three modes determined by source-text
availability. For the Lexical Title Unpacking mode (Urdu canon),
the model has no source plot to memorise; the question of
plot-level memorisation is therefore ill-defined for that
category. This subsection complements the TRR analysis by asking
the question that does apply to the remaining categories: when a
source text \emph{is} plausibly present in the training data
(global fables and Western literary classics), to what extent
does the model reproduce its plot, theme and resolution? We
restrict this analysis to the Fable and English Literature
categories of Section~\ref{subsec:title_repetition}.

\paragraph{Three-axis structural fingerprint.}
We characterise each (original, generated) story pair along
three independent narratological axes, judged by GPT-4o-mini at
temperature~0 against the original story:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Plot overlap} -- the fraction of the original's
        abstract plot beats (4--8 character-agnostic events
        extracted from the original) that recur in the generated
        story.
  \item \textbf{Theme overlap} -- semantic similarity of the
        central messages of the two stories.
  \item \textbf{Resolution match} -- semantic similarity of the
        one-sentence narrative endings, compared in isolation.
\end{itemize}

Each axis is mapped to a boolean using fixed thresholds
(plot~$\ge$~0.60, theme~$\ge$~0.70, resolution~$\ge$~0.80) and a
generated story is classified as memorisation when at least two
of the three axes agree. Crossed with character overlap, this
yields a 2$\times$2 typology
(Table~\ref{tab:memorisation_typology}):

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Faithful Retelling} -- high structural overlap
        \emph{with} original characters preserved (acknowledged
        memorisation, e.g.\ the canonical Thirsty Crow).
  \item \textbf{Disguised Memorisation} -- high structural
        overlap \emph{with} characters renamed (e.g.\ the greedy
        dog rewritten as a greedy cat, plot and moral intact).
  \item \textbf{Spin-off} -- low overlap, same characters reused
        in a new plot (rare).
  \item \textbf{Original} -- low overlap, new characters.
\end{itemize}

The \emph{Disguised Memorisation} cell is the cell of interest:
it identifies cases in which the model has reproduced a
memorised plot while applying cosmetic renaming, which would
otherwise be missed by surface-level overlap metrics.

\paragraph{Findings.}
Across the Fable and English Literature subsets
(n~=~XX titles), QWEN exhibits the highest disguised
memorisation rate (XX\%), followed by DeepSeek (XX\%) and GPT
(XX\%). Faithful retellings dominate the Fable subcategory for
all three models, consistent with the near-zero TRR observed
there: when a memorised plot is available, the model retrieves
it and does not need to repeat the title to maintain coherence.
The English Literature subcategory shows the opposite pattern,
with thematic reproduction dominating: plot and resolution
overlap are moderate, theme overlap is high, and character
overlap is low. This is the structural signature of an LLM that
has read \emph{about} a story (plot summaries, critical
commentary) without having memorised the story itself.

\paragraph{Cross-section coherence.}
The findings of this subsection and of
Section~\ref{subsec:title_repetition} converge on a single
picture. When the source text is present in training data
(Fables, English Literature), the model reproduces it
structurally and the title fades into the background
(low~TRR, high plot/theme/resolution overlap). When the source
text is absent (Urdu Literature), there is no structure to
reproduce and the title itself becomes the anchor (high TRR, no
meaningful plot-level reproduction). The Disguised Memorisation
sub-finding and the Title Lexicalization Effect are therefore
two faces of the same underlying constraint: contemporary LLMs
generate Urdu stories conditional on the presence of the
specific source text in their training corpus, and their
behaviour gracefully degrades from faithful reproduction
through thematic paraphrase to lexical title-unpacking as that
presence diminishes.

\subsection{Cross-Model Structural Analysis}
\label{sec:networks}

So far we have looked at sentence-level errors, cultural
mismatches, and how the three LLMs engage with their source
titles. In this section we step back from individual sentences
and ask whether the three models produce stories with similar
social structures, or whether each model has its own style at
the level of how characters relate to one another.

We adopt the signed character network methodology
of~\citep{networkpaper2025}, which was originally applied to
English LLM stories, and apply it to our Urdu corpus. For every
story we asked GPT-4o-mini at temperature zero to list the
characters and the relationships between them, labelling each
relationship as positive (affection, friendship, love),
negative (conflict, hostility, betrayal), or neutral
(acquaintance, professional, functional) with an intensity from
1 to 3. We then built a graph in which characters are nodes and
relationships are edges, and computed structural measures:
number of characters and relationships, network density,
average clustering, the positivity ratio (positive divided by
signed edges), and the mean signed edge weight (intensity
scaled to the range $[-1, +1]$). We restrict the analysis to
the three LLM corpora, each producing 31 stories of
approximately 2{,}000 words. We explain at the end of the
subsection why a direct comparison against the human corpus is
not included.

This network analysis was not exploratory. During the
linguistic and cultural annotation pass
(Section~\ref{subsec:linguistic_errors}), the annotators kept
an informal reading log of qualitative impressions of each
story. The log repeatedly noted that the generated stories
felt unusually warm and that QWEN's stories felt the warmest
of all. The network analysis described below was designed
specifically to test whether these informal impressions
corresponded to a measurable structural signal.

\paragraph{The three models write small, similar-sized worlds.}
On average DeepSeek produces 4.8 characters and 4.2
relationships per story, GPT produces 4.3 characters and 3.4
relationships, and QWEN produces 4.0 characters and 3.3
relationships. Network density is highest for GPT (0.64) and
lowest for DeepSeek (0.52), with QWEN in between (0.58),
meaning that GPT casts tend to be more fully connected than
the other two. Most stories form a single connected social
group rather than parallel subplots, with the mean number of
connected components close to 1 for all models.
Figure~\ref{fig:density_violin} shows the full distribution of
density across stories.

\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{figures/fig3_density_violin.pdf}
\caption{Distribution of character network density across 31
stories per model. Each black dot is a single story, the white

\paragraph{All three models are systematically too positive.}
During the annotation phase, before any network metric was
computed, the annotators informally noticed that the generated
stories felt unusually upbeat. Characters reconciled, families
bonded, and strangers became friends with a frequency that did
not match either their reading of canonical Urdu fiction or
their intuition about realistic social interaction. Among the
three models, QWEN stood out as the most aggressively warm,
with even conflict-laden source titles often resolving into
mutual understanding. The network analysis confirms this
observation quantitatively. When we restrict attention to
relationships with real emotional content and ignore neutral
acquaintances, the positivity ratio is 0.79 for DeepSeek, 0.85
for GPT, and 0.87 for QWEN. Out of every ten emotionally
charged relationships in these stories, eight or nine are
friendly or supportive and only one or two involve conflict.
The median positivity ratio is 1.0 for all three models,
meaning that more than half of all generated stories contain
no negative relationship at all
(Figure~\ref{fig:positivity_violin}). The model ordering the
annotators perceived (QWEN $>$ GPT $>$ DeepSeek) matches the
metric ordering exactly. This replicates on Urdu the positivity
bias that~\citep{networkpaper2025} reported for English LLM
stories, and suggests that the bias is a property of
contemporary LLM training rather than of any particular source
language.

\caption{Distribution of positivity ratio (positive divided by

\paragraph{QWEN amplifies emotional intensity.}
The annotators also independently noticed, in the same reading
pass, that QWEN's positive relationships did not feel merely
positive but \emph{intensely} positive: lifelong devotion in
place of friendship, profound transformation in place of modest
reconciliation. The metric agrees. The mean signed edge weight
is 0.37 for DeepSeek, 0.45 for GPT, and 0.49 for QWEN. The
unsigned average follows the same ordering at 1.10, 1.26, and
1.37. QWEN does not only add positive edges, it dials them up.
Relationships in QWEN stories are more often portrayed as deep
and devoted rather than casual or moderate
(Figure~\ref{fig:intensity_violin}). This is consistent with
our earlier finding (Section~\ref{subsec:memorization}) that
QWEN shows the highest disguised memorisation rate. The same
model that most strongly anchors to source-style plots also
produces the most emotionally polarised relationships.

\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{figures/fig2_intensity_violin.pdf}
\caption{Distribution of mean signed edge weight per story on

\label{fig:intensity_violin}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{The three models invent almost entirely different
characters from the same prompt.}
For each of the 31 shared prompts we computed the Jaccard
overlap of the character sets produced by every pair of
models. The numbers are striking. The mean overlap is 0.025
for GPT and QWEN, 0.021 for GPT and DeepSeek, and 0.019 for
DeepSeek and QWEN. The median overlap is exactly zero for
every pair, meaning that for more than half of all prompts no
two models named even a single character in common
(Figure~\ref{fig:jaccard_overlap}). Even with the same Urdu
title and the same word budget, the three models populate the
story with completely different people. This tells us two
things. First, the structural patterns we report above
(positivity, intensity) are not driven by all three models
pulling characters from the same shared template. Each model
has its own preferences. Second, if these three models were
used together to generate synthetic Urdu training data, they
would explore very different parts of the character space
rather than reinforcing the same narrative templates.

\paragraph{Why we do not compare to human stories here.}
A natural question is how these LLM networks compare against
human-authored Urdu short stories. We chose not to make that
comparison in this section, and we want to be open about why.
Our human reference stories are published Urdu short fiction
by authors such as Manto, Chughtai, Bedi, and Krishan Chander,
and they are typically 5{,}000 to 15{,}000 words long. Our LLM
stories are constrained to roughly 2{,}000 words. A longer
story naturally has room for more incidental and functional
characters such as shopkeepers, neighbours, and passing
acquaintances. These characters add to the total count and
appear in the network as neutral edges. A shorter story simply
does not have room for them. A direct human-vs-LLM comparison
on character counts or neutral edge proportions would
therefore be confounded by length rather than by authorship.
We considered truncating the human stories to 2{,}000 words
but rejected this option, because it would discard the
resolution and closing character introductions of most
published short stories and replace a length confound with a
worse truncation artefact. We therefore restrict the network
analysis to comparisons between the LLMs, where every model
operates under the same generation constraints, and leave a
controlled length-matched human-LLM comparison for future
work.

\section{Limitations}
\label{subsec:trr_limit}
%─────────────────────────────────────────

Exact-phrase TRR may underestimate title anchoring when 
models decompose multi-word titles. GPT's \foreignlanguage{arabic}{گرم کوٹ} 
(TRR~=~0.16) illustrates this: the exact phrase appeared 
only 3 times, yet \foreignlanguage{arabic}{کوٹ} alone was used extensively 
throughout --- depicting a character with a \foreignlanguage{arabic}{شال} 
aspiring to own a \foreignlanguage{arabic}{گرم کوٹ}. Partial-word matching 
and synonym detection represent directions for future 
refinement of the TRR metric.
